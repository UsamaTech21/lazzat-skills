# Canadian Food Advertising Law - Lazzat Reference

Authoritative claims that Lazzat writes, speaks, or prints are governed by four overlapping legal frameworks. Both **literal meaning** and **general impression** are considered in enforcement. Burden of proof sits with the advertiser - us.

---

## The four laws that govern every Lazzat claim

### 1. Food and Drugs Act (FDA)

- **Section 5(1)** - Prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive labelling and advertising of all food. Applies at every level of trade, including restaurants.
- **Section 3(1)-(2)** - Prohibits therapeutic claims on food. Any food making a health or healing claim is reclassified as a drug, triggering drug-level regulatory requirements.

**Implication for Lazzat:** Any wall panel, menu line, or ad copy implying that our food heals, detoxifies, boosts immunity, improves energy, or treats any condition is a drug claim. The salt wall is the highest-risk area - a single misplaced claim about negative ions or air purification turns decorative stone into a regulated medical device.

### 2. Safe Food for Canadians Act (SFCA)

- **Section 6(1)** defines "advertisement" broadly: *"a representation by any means for the purpose of promoting directly or indirectly the sale of a food commodity."*

**Implication for Lazzat:** Wall signage, menus, window decals, social posts, film, delivery-app descriptions, hashtag use, influencer briefs we write, and schema.org markup visible in Google search results all count as advertising. CFIA can act on anything visible to a customer.

### 3. Competition Act

- **Section 52** - Prohibits representations to the public that are false or misleading in a material respect.
- **Section 74.01** - Requires performance or efficacy claims to be based on "adequate and proper testing." **Burden of proof is on the advertiser.**

**Key rule:** "Both the general impression AND the literal meaning are considered. It is not necessary to prove anyone was actually deceived."

**Implication for Lazzat:** If we claim "lowest real-fruit shake in Brampton," we must have current evidence that no competitor is priced lower. If we claim "200g per plate," every plate must actually be 200g or more.

### 4. Canadian Code of Advertising Standards (Ad Standards Canada)

Applies to advertising in any medium. Any consumer can file a complaint. Clause 1 requires accuracy and substantiation.

**Implication:** A single customer complaint can trigger a Standards Council review.

## Specific claims - safe vs risky

### Chicken and meat

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Maple Lodge Farms chicken" | Low | Supplier identification - safe, defensible, replaces "halal" framing for front-of-house. |
| "Raised without added hormones" | Medium | Must include the disclaimer: "Like other chickens, this chicken was raised without added hormones." |
| "Hormone-free" | HIGH | Illegal framing. Growth hormones have been banned in Canadian poultry since 1963. CFIA explicitly rejects this phrase. |
| "Antibiotic-free" | Medium | Requires documented testing; "raised without the use of antibiotics" is safer wording. |
| "Halal" on customer-facing surfaces | HIGH (brand risk) | Not a legal ban - a Lazzat brand policy. See Section 8 of this skill's `SKILL.md`. |

### Freshness

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Made fresh on premises" | Low | Safe if true - drinks blended to order, sauces made in-house. |
| "Real fruit" | Low | Defensible - we use real IQF whole fruit. |
| "Fresh fruit" | HIGH | Frozen is not fresh under CFIA. Do not use. |
| "Natural" | Medium | Regulated term. Undefined in practice. Invites challenge. |
| "Organic" (without Canada Organic certification) | HIGH | Certification is mandatory to use the term. |

### Health and wellness

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Immunity / Detox / Superfood" | VERY HIGH | Drug-adjacent claims. Up to $10M CFIA fine potential per incident. |
| "Healthy" | HIGH | Nutrient content claim under Food and Drug Regulations. |
| "Low calorie / low fat" | Medium | Only if verified per FDR thresholds and disclosed. |

### Salt wall

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Approximately 600 million years old" | Low | Verified in peer-reviewed literature (Khan et al., 2026). |
| "800 million" / "850 million" | Medium-High | Not scientifically supported for the salt itself. |
| "Rich in 84 essential minerals" | HIGH | Misleading - trace minerals, not nutritionally significant. |
| "Naturally occurring minerals" | Low | Safer substitute phrasing. |
| "Purifies air / negative ions / detoxifies" | VERY HIGH | Therapeutic claim → drug reclassification. Directly refuted by Salt Therapy Association. |
| "Healthier than table salt" | HIGH | Not supported at normal consumption levels. |

### Pricing and comparison

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Honest pricing" | Low | Subjective positioning, not a factual comparison. |
| "Lowest real-fruit shake in Brampton" | Medium | Requires ongoing price verification against competitors. Keep evidence on file. |
| "X% less than [Competitor]" | HIGH | Comparative claims require current substantiation; competitor could challenge. Also violates brand's "no comparison" rule. Do not print. |
| "Better than [Competitor]" | HIGH | Same as above + defamation exposure. |

### Allergens

| Claim | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Contains: Dairy, Gluten" | Low | Required on delivery platforms; safest format. |
| "Dairy-free option available" | Low | Safe if true and procedurally enforced. |
| "Nut-free kitchen" | Medium | Only if fully verified with zero cross-contact protocol. |
| "Gluten-free" | HIGH | Zero-tolerance cross-contact. Use "gluten-friendly" if unsure. |
| "Allergen-free" | VERY HIGH | Absolute claim. Never use. |

## Ontario-specific rules

- **Menu labelling law:** Calorie disclosure is mandatory only for chains with 20+ Ontario locations. Lazzat is currently below this threshold - but be aware that if the brand grows, retrofitting calorie counts on screens and menus is non-trivial.
- **Bilingual labelling:** Not required in Ontario at the restaurant level. English-only is fine.

## The one question that catches everything

**"If a journalist asked us to prove this claim tomorrow morning, could we?"**

- Yes → keep.
- No → rewrite.
- Maybe → rewrite.

---

## Escalation path

If a claim is uncertain, the decision tree is:

1. Can the claim be stated as a verifiable fact? → Use it.
2. Is the claim subjective positioning (e.g., "honest")? → Use sparingly, never as a comparison.
3. Is the claim about a competitor? → Remove. No exceptions.
4. Is the claim about health, detox, immunity, or any bodily effect? → Remove. No exceptions.
5. Is the claim about our own existing restaurants (Osmow's / sister brands) - positive or negative? → Frame as "we learned" only.
